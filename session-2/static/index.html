Why these numbers?

Fertility = tokens ÷ faithful units (letter/number runs + visible punctuation). Score = 1000 / (max fertility − min fertility) — it rewards being equally efficient across English, Hindi, Telugu and Tamil, not being efficient in an absolute sense.

Why the score is tied to this dataset: training uses one Wikipedia article per language (the "India" page). BPE merges are chosen by counting the most frequent byte-pairs in that corpus, so words that don't appear there — like "hello" — never earn a dedicated merge and fall back to fragments, even though the trained score above is good. Indic scripts also need more merges per word than English (conjuncts, matras, a larger effective alphabet), so a shared 10k-token budget split four ways leaves them behind before rebalancing even starts.

Why you can't just "fix" BPE: merge selection is greedy frequency counting with no built-in notion of fairness. The auto-balance rounds are a workaround bolted on top — retrain from scratch each round with adjusted per-language weights — not a change to BPE itself, so it converges to a locally good spread, not a perfect one.

To get better numbers: (1) use a broader corpus per language, not one topic page — biggest lever; (2) raise vocab size (try closer to 20,000); (3) raise auto-balance rounds (try closer to 16); (4) test on text representative of the training domain — a single out-of-corpus word like "hello" isn't a fair comparison to the trained score.
